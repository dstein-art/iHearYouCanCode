1. Map oscillator frequencies to the screen

Start with several sine oscillators at random pitches. Each frame, draw a green line where that oscillator's frequency lands on the canvas — low notes near the bottom, high notes near the top. Click to pick new random frequencies.

This is the ground truth: you know what frequencies you're playing because you set them with osc.freq(). Later you'll try to figure out frequencies just by listening and analyzing.

2. Analyze frequencies with p5.FFT

Given any audio signal, how do you recover the frequencies inside it? A Fast Fourier Transform (p5.FFT) breaks sound into chunks of the frequency spectrum. Call fft.analyze() each frame, then use fft.getEnergy(hz) to ask how loud a frequency range is.

Grey bars show what the FFT guesses. Green lines show the real oscillator frequencies. They mostly match — but FFT results are a bit chunky because the algorithm uses evenly-spaced bins instead of exact pitch numbers.

3. Draw the frequency spectrum

Any sound — not just oscillators — can be drawn as a bar graph across the frequency spectrum. Load a sound file, loop it, and use the values from fft.analyze() directly. Each bar is one FFT bin: low bins on the left, high bins on the right.

You can change the number of bins in the constructor — it must be a power of 2 (64, 128, 256, 512, or 1024). Fewer bins = chunkier but faster to draw.

4. Draw the waveform

fft.waveform() looks at a tiny slice of time instead of frequency. It returns values between -1 and 1 — the shape of the wave at this moment. Connect the dots to draw the classic squiggly line you see in audio editors.

5. Build a spectrogram

A spectrogram shows how frequencies change over time. Each column is one snapshot of the spectrum; scroll across the screen to paint a history — brighter means louder at that frequency.

This is the slit-scan technique from the Week 2 notes: use bin values as fill color and step across the canvas one column at a time.

Analyzing loudness with p5.Amplitude

Before FFT, there's an even simpler question: how loud is the signal right now? p5.Amplitude averages a short slice of the wave and returns a level from 0 to 1 with getLevel(). Hook it up with amp.setInput(mic) or amp.setInput(soundFile). 

var mic, amp;

mic = new p5.AudioIn();
mic.start();

amp = new p5.Amplitude();
amp.setInput(mic);

var level = amp.getLevel();
// map level to the height of a bar in draw()

For microphone sketches, call fft.setInput(mic) or amp.setInput(mic) so analysis follows the mic instead of the sketch's main output. Mic access may not work inside the live preview — run in the p5.js Editor and allow microphone permission.

Handy FFT methods

  • fft.analyze() — frequency spectrum array (0–255 per bin)
  • fft.waveform() — time-domain wave shape (-1 to 1)
  • fft.getEnergy(hz) or getEnergy('bass') — energy at one frequency or range
  • fft.setInput(source) — analyze a specific sound or mic instead of the main output
  • new p5.FFT(smoothing, bins) — smoothing 0–1; bins must be a power of 2
